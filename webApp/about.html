Introduction

We performed a computational analysis on the first 70 aa at the N-t (N-terminal analysis) of the whole proteome or on 70 aa windows starting from all internal TMDs (Internal TMDs analysis) to identify potential SPC cleavage sites not associated with signal peptides (cryptic cleavage sites) by using a pre-existing a signal peptide prediction software (SignalP) and comparing output of two networks (TM and noTM). Our hits represent membrane protein with a type-II oriented TMD predicted to be cleaved in noTM but not in TM. Moreover, we rank the hits based on the number of disease-linked mutation.

Commptational method

We used the command-line version of the SignalP4.1 program. The program takes a sequence (FASTA formatted) of 70 amino acids length as input and predicts the cleavage site and reports the Y-score (combined cleavage site score) in its output. We retrieved all the proteins and their associated feature information from UniProt/Swiss-Prot. We modified the SignalP command-line version to forced our datasets selectively in SignalP_TM and SignalP_noTM version (see methods for details). To investigate the proteins with cryptic cleavage sites, we created two types of peptide sequences (FASTA formatted) with the first 70 amino acids of all the: (i) proteins that lack a canonical signal peptide N-terminal analysis, (ii) transmembrane domains of proteins that lack a canonical signal peptide Internal TMDs analysis. We predicted the Y-scores of both types of peptide sequences using the SignalP-TM and SignalP-noTM version. A protein/transmembrane domain was considered to have a non-canonical cleavage site if its corresponding peptideâ€™s Y-score in the SignalP-TM output was less than 0.6 and in SignalP- noTM output was more than 0.5. Finally, we assigned mutational information derived from (a) UniProt, COSMIC, and ClinVAR.

Validation experiments

Validation of the hits were then performed by ectopically expressing wt and mutant constructs in wt HEK293T cells or lacking the regulatory SPC subunit SPCS1 and cell lysates were then analysed via Western blot to detect possible SPCS1-dependent cleavage fragments (see methods for details).
